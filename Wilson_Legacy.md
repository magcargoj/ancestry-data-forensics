# Wilson Viking & Pioneer Legacy

This document summarizes the historical and genetic origins of the Wilson line, tracing the family from the ancient Norse settlements to the pioneers of Pitt County, North Carolina.

## 1. The Ancient Norse & Viking Roots
The surname **Wilson** ("son of Will") is a patronymic name that emerged in the **Danelaw** region of Northern England and the **Scottish Lowlands**. These areas were the heart of Viking settlement in the 9th-11th centuries.

*   **Clan Gunn Connection**: In Scotland, the Wilsons are recognized as a sept of **Clan Gunn**. The clan claims descent from **Gunni**, a grandson of **Sweyn Asleifsson**, famously called the **"Ultimate Viking"** in the *Orkneyinga Sagas*.
*   **The Wolf Symbol**: The frequent use of the wolf in Wilson heraldry is believed to commemorate a 9th-century Danish prince named **Wolf** who settled in the Orkney Islands.

## 2. The Scots-Irish Migration
In the 17th century, many Wilson families moved from Scotland to **Ulster (Northern Ireland)** as part of the plantation settlements. In the mid-18th century (c. 1740–1770), they migrated to the American colonies.

*   **Arrival in NC**: The Pitt County Wilsons likely arrived via the **Fall Line Road** from Virginia or through the ports of New Bern and Wilmington.
*   **Settlement**: They were among the earliest European farmers in the **Swift Creek** and **Grindle Creek** areas of Pitt County, along the Tar River.

## 3. Revolutionary Patriots
The family has a proud record of service in the American Revolution:
*   **Thomas Wilson**: Served in the **North Carolina Third Regiment**.
*   **Willis Wilson (1752–1833)**: A Pitt County pioneer and Revolutionary private.
*   **Nashville Wilson (b. 1815)**: Named during the era of Westward expansion, his name likely honors the "Nashville" settlement (named for NC General Francis Nash).
